Film Review - The Women

Opening scene: A tiny yorkie goes berserk at another dog while on a walk (common experience for small dog owners). After flashing its sharp teeth, it is scooped up by an obviously wealthy socialite, who coddles and calls the yorkie “precious” and sneers and calls the other (simply passing) dog “nasty.”

And thus the premise is set.

“The Women” could easily be called “The 1930s’ Real Housewives of New York”. (But with good writing!) I found the ensemble fascinating. The script works hard to ensure you are never bored and that the absence of men feels natural.

Final scene: Crystal’s parting words, “There’s a name for you ladies, but it isn't used in high society… outside a kennel!” Well, Mary is indeed that bitch. After her vicious snap at nasty Crystal succeeds in driving her off, Mary is swept back into the embrace of her husband and the high society status he symbolizes— where she is always loved, and always excused.

Film Review - Gone Girl

I’ve been craving more of Gillian Flynn’s work, and it was either read the book again or finally watch this film.

Unlike Dark Places and Sharp Objects, Gone Girl lets its plot twists appear in the center of the plot rather than at the very end. Every event plays like a chess match move between Nick and Amy. (I say the book is better, because Amy and Nick’s initial motivations were not communicated at all in the film. Plus, knowing their exact thoughts enrich the ending.)

Nevertheless the film adapts everything else that is important to the story while also being masterfully produced and entertaining.

Major props for Tyler Perry’s delivery of the line “You two are the most fucked up people I ever met, and I specialize in fucked up people”

Film Review - The Power of the Dog

One of my favorite films of 2021.

Like the book, this is a story told through hands. The comb. The crafts. A gentle hand placed on her shoulder as she's crying. No gloves. New gloves. All are moments lifted from the text and with the addition of a brilliant new scene: Peter flipping though his medical textbook, briefly pausing on the page that illustrates the vulnerable areas of the palms...

Benny C's uncanny cowboy drawl could have ruined this film! But the direction, the cinematography, elevates the story to something bigger than the characters. It's deliberate and perfect.

Tragic when art imitates life

Chrome yellow has a nasty habit of browning as it ages. Research carried out on Van Gogh's paintings in Amsterdam over the past few years has shown that some of the chrome yellow in his flower petals have darkened significantly, due to the reaction of chrome yellow with other pigments in sunlight. Van Gogh's sunflowers, it seems, are wilting, just as their real-life counterparts did.

(Source: The Secret Lives of Color)

Excerpt - Red Dragon

Sometimes at night I would leave the lights on in my little house and walk across the flat fields. When I looked back from a distance, the house looked like a boat at sea, and all around me the vast Delta night. I soon became acquainted with the semi-feral dogs who roamed free across the fields in what was more or less a pack. Some of them had casual arrangements with the families of farm workers, but much of the time they had to forage for themselves. In the hard winter months with the ground frozen and dry, I started giving them dog food and soon they were going through fifty pounds of dog food a week. They walked with me in the fields at night and when I couldn't see them, I could hear them all around me, breathing and snuffling along in the dark. When I was working in the cabin, they waited on the font porch, and when the moon was full they would sing...
